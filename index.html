Chapter 1: In the Beginning

What I Did

Growing up across multiple cities in India before moving to Ireland gave me a unique perspective on life and learning. When I took the Leaving Certificate, I pushed myself hard and achieved 613 points—a score I was genuinely proud of. But what really mattered to me wasn't just the number; it was what I was building on the side.

At 13, I started taking software development courses, not because anyone told me to, but because I was curious. I spent hours building games and experimenting with Python and Java, just for the fun of it. Then came the drone project. A friend from across the world and I decided to buy a cheap 3D printer, ship it to Ireland, and build a drone from scratch. We had no idea what we were doing, but we figured it out together.

I initially thought I'd go into mechanical engineering—I loved the hands-on building. But as we assembled that drone, I realized something: I wasn't just interested in how things moved. I wanted to understand how they thought, how they made decisions. That's when I discovered electronics wasn't just a subject—it was where hardware met intelligence, and that became my path forward.

Genuine Reflections

Moving between cultures taught me to adapt and see problems from different perspectives. At 13, programming was just fun—I had no idea it would become my career. Building that drone was transformative. I loved the mechanical process, the hands-on building, but ultimately realized my heart was in making things think, not just move. Electronics was where hardware met intelligence, and that's where I found my calling.

Chapter 2: Found My Love for Engineering

What I Did

When I decided on engineering, it felt like the obvious choice given my interests. But choosing it and loving it are two different things. I only truly fell in love with engineering when I joined Formula Trinity Autonomous in 2021.

I started working on control systems—PID controllers, Model Predictive Control (MPC), Pure Pursuit algorithms—the kind of stuff that makes an autonomous car actually drive. It was challenging, often frustrating, but incredibly rewarding. By 2022-23, I was leading the control department, essentially architecting the decision-making brain of our autonomous vehicle. Then in 2024, I became Captain of FT AI, leading the entire autonomous systems team.

But here's the thing about Formula Student: it's not easy. We spent three long years struggling at FSUK competitions, facing failure after failure. And then, in 2024 at Silverstone, everything came together. We completed our first lap. We won the award for Best Design. And we won the award I'm most proud of: Most Cones Knocked. That last one might sound funny, but it represents every mistake, every iteration, every lesson learned the hard way.

Genuine Reflections

When I decided on engineering, it was the obvious choice given my interests. But I only truly fell in love with it when I joined Formula Trinity in 2021. This is one of the proudest achievements of my life—not just because we won awards, but because of the journey. Three years of failures, late nights, debugging, and rebuilding. When we finally completed that lap at Silverstone, I felt every second of those three years was worth it. I built everlasting relationships and friendships that I'll carry forever. The "Most Cones Knocked" award? That's our battle scar, our reminder that perfection isn't the goal—progress is.

Chapter 3: Early Research

What I Did

  • Laidlaw Research and Leadership Scholarship - Awarded the prestigious scholarship to conduct independent research
  • Research Focus - Under the supervision of Prof. Harun Siljak, investigated adaptive network systems and designed an adaptive model to control and stabilize complex networks with a minimal number of adaptive network nodes
    • p>Being awarded the Laidlaw Research and Leadership Scholarship was a turning point. It wasn't just an honor—it was an opportunity to pursue my own research ideas with the guidance of someone brilliant.

    I worked under the supervision of Prof. Harun Siljak, investigating adaptive network systems. The core question we explored was fascinating: Can you control and stabilize a complex network by introducing just a few adaptive, controllable nodes instead of trying to control everything? It's a bit like asking whether you can influence a crowd by strategically placing a few key people rather than trying to direct everyone.

    We designed adaptive models, ran simulations, and published our findings in research reflections and posters. The work showed that yes, you can increase both autonomy and synchronization in complex systems with minimal intervention. It was elegant, almost counterintuitive, and deeply satisfying to prove.

    Research taught me patience and humility. You can spend weeks on an idea only to prove it doesn't work—and that's progress too. Working with Prof. Siljak opened my eyes to the elegance of complex systems. The idea that you don't need to control everything to achieve stability—that a few strategic adaptive nodes can synchronize an entire network—felt almost philosophical. It changed how I think about control, not just in networks, but in life.

What I Did

  • Local Pub - Needed pocket money while studying full-time in Dublin. Quickly realized this was a job I couldn't handle and gained immense respect for those who do it every day
    • p>Living and studying full-time in Dublin meant I needed some way to make pocket money. So naturally, I thought, "How hard can working at a pub be?" The answer: very hard. I quickly realized it was a job I couldn't handle. The pace, the chaos of a busy Friday night, the constant pressure—I gained immense respect for everyone in hospitality who does this every day.

    I then joined RampInfoTech Ltd, a startup providing digital transformation, automation, and AI tools to companies. I worked as a client-facing developer and product manager, which meant I wasn't just writing code—I was talking to customers, understanding their problems, and designing tools to make their lives more efficient. I built a Container Tracking Automation system that reduced over 400 hours of manual work, tracking 4000+ large shipments in real-time. I also developed a Travel and Expense Management tool for internal use. Along the way, I learned Python, MS Power Tools (Power BI, Power Apps), SQL, and API development. More importantly, I learned how to communicate effectively and collaborate with larger onshore and offshore teams.

    Then I traveled to Zagreb, Croatia to work with Radiona.org, a non-profit dedicated to enabling and connecting tech enthusiasts, hackers, and learners. I contributed to open-source FPGA development with the goal of reducing the digital divide in hardware engineering. Coming from a software and robotics background, I'd seen how access to powerful tools often determines who gets to innovate. This project gave me the opportunity to change that.

    I worked on the ULX3S low-cost FPGA board, designing and verifying digital logic in Verilog and adapting a self-trained super-resolution deep learning model to run on resource-constrained Lattice ECP5 FPGAs. Through simulation and synthesis, I learned how to translate algorithms into efficient hardware implementations. Beyond the technical work, I helped build accessible resources for first-time FPGA developers, enabling advanced computation on affordable, open hardware.

    Genuine Reflections

    That pub job humbled me fast. I thought I could handle anything—turns out I couldn't handle the chaos of a busy Dublin pub on a Friday night. Respect to everyone in hospitality. Moving to RampInfoTech was eye-opening. I wasn't just writing code anymore; I was solving real problems for real people. Seeing a tool I built save hundreds of hours made me understand the difference between code that works and code that matters. Then Croatia happened. Working at Radiona.org wasn't just about FPGAs—it was about democratizing technology. Coming from a software and robotics background, I'd seen how access determines who innovates. This project let me change that. Making powerful tools accessible and affordable? That's engineering with purpose.

Chapter 5: Getting Deeper into Cars

What I Did

  • Jaguar Land Rover - Placement - Joined the Assisted and Autonomous Driving team, specifically the AI Onboard Perception team
  • Edge-Based R&D - Developed niche perception tools and features using onboard vehicle sensors. Daily routine included reading research papers, discussing and developing IPs/Patents, writing high-quality optimized code for data engineering, synthetic dataset generation, AI/ML model training and optimization
  • Multi-Sensor Fusion Visualization - Built a simulation tool to visualize large datasets with all sensors (camera, lidar, radar, ultrasonic) in one singular view for edge case identification, fault detection, labeling, and detection
  • ACES Challenge - Winner - In JLR's undergraduate competition, developed a mesh-based distributed network to connect high-security convoy vehicles for automated re-routing, threat detection, communication, and security. Built a simulated proof of concept and showcased a real-world vehicular demonstration. Won the competition and received the award for Most Innovative

Starting my placement at Jaguar Land Rover felt like stepping into the future. I joined the Assisted and Autonomous Driving team, specifically working within the AI Onboard Perception group. This was edge-based R&D at its finest—we developed niche, innovative perception tools and features using the car's onboard sensors.

My daily routine was unlike anything I'd experienced before. I'd start by reading cutting-edge research papers, then spend time discussing and developing intellectual property and patents with my team. The rest of the day involved writing high-quality, optimized code for data engineering, synthetic dataset generation, and AI/ML model training and optimization. I was mentored by incredibly knowledgeable individuals who genuinely wanted to teach me, and I learned so much from them.

One of my main contributions was building a multi-sensor fusion visualization simulation tool. It allowed us to visualize massive datasets from all sensors—cameras, lidar, radar, and ultrasonic—in one unified view. This made it possible to identify edge cases, detect faults, and improve our labeling and detection systems.

Then came the ACES Challenge, JLR's undergraduate competition. We were tasked with developing an innovative idea for vehicles. My team and I built a mesh-based distributed network to connect high-security convoy vehicles for automated re-routing, threat detection, communication, and security. We created a simulated proof of concept and showcased a real-world vehicular demonstration. We won the competition and were given the award for Most Innovative.

Genuine Reflections

I'm currently in my final year of Electronic & Computer Engineering at Trinity College Dublin—it's my Masters year, and it feels like everything I've learned is coming together.

For my thesis, I'm working on transistor parasitic model extraction for GaN FETs (Gallium Nitride Field-Effect Transistors) to improve device modeling accuracy. It's highly specialized work that combines hardware design, simulation, and precision engineering—all the things I've grown passionate about over the years.

Alongside my thesis, I'm also developing a Vision Transformer for a hybrid Quantum Deep Learning network as part of my coursework. It's cutting-edge, experimental, and honestly a bit intimidating—but that's what makes it exciting. I'm pushing into territory I don't fully understand yet, and that's exactly where I want to be.
